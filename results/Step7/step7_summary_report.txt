1. RESEARCH QUESTION
--------------------------------------------------

  When should semiconductor companies invest in sophisticated
  stochastic (uncertainty-aware) planning versus simpler
  deterministic (average-yield) planning?

  Gap in the literature: Existing work shows that stochastic
  planning can outperform deterministic planning under yield
  uncertainty, but provides no quantitative thresholds or
  decision rules for practitioners.

3. KEY FINDINGS
--------------------------------------------------

  Finding 1: Front-End yield uncertainty is the PRIMARY driver
  of the planning approach choice.
    - FE uncertainty produces increasing VSS because recourse
      actions (outsourcing, inventory adjustment) are available
      after FE yields are revealed.
    - BE uncertainty hurts both approaches equally ('no recourse'
      property at the final manufacturing stage).
    - Evidence: Steps 3 and 4 (45 experiments each)

  Finding 2: Deterministic planning fails at a specific threshold.
    - When FE yield spread exceeds ~0.18, deterministic planning
      loses >2% of profit compared to stochastic planning.
    - Customer service degrades via a 'cliff effect' - stable
      until the threshold, then rapid deterioration.
    - Evidence: Step 5 (150 experiments, 5 failure criteria)

  Finding 3: The failure threshold is ROBUST to cost changes.
    - Across penalty/price ratios from 10% to 80%, the critical
      threshold stays in the narrow range 0.17–0.18.
    - Service-based thresholds are perfectly cost-independent.
    - Cost-based thresholds shift modestly: higher penalties push
      the threshold slightly lower (stochastic needed sooner).
    - Evidence: Step 6 (200 experiments, 4 cost scenarios)

  Finding 4: The hedging mechanism operates through wafer allocation,
  not inventory buffering.
    - Stochastic planning allocates more wafer starts to the
      secondary FE facility (FE2) as insurance.
    - Die-bank inventory is near zero for both approaches
      (driven by 60:1 penalty-to-holding-cost ratio).
    - Evidence: Steps 3 and 4

  PRIMARY RULE:

5. CONTRIBUTIONS TO THE LITERATURE
--------------------------------------------------

  Contribution 1: Quantitative failure thresholds.
    Prior work (Rashidi et al.) shows VSS grows with uncertainty
    but does not identify where it becomes consequential.
    We provide the specific yield spread values (0.17–0.18)
    where the planning approach choice becomes financially
    significant (>2% of profit at stake).

  Contribution 2: Cost-structure sensitivity analysis.
    We show that the threshold is robust across penalty/price
    ratios from 10% to 80%, shifting by only 0.01 units of
    yield spread. This means the decision rule is broadly
    applicable, not specific to one cost scenario.

  Contribution 3: Uncertainty decomposition.
    We establish that FE and BE uncertainty play fundamentally
    different roles: FE drives the planning choice (recourse),
    BE drives overall profit impact (no recourse). This
    decomposition guides practitioners toward the right response:
    upgrade planning for FE issues, upgrade quality for BE issues.

  Contribution 4: Practitioner-ready decision tool.
    The decision map (Figure 1) and lookup table provide a
    ready-to-use tool that requires only two inputs from the
    practitioner: their FE yield variability and their shortage
    cost structure.


6. LIMITATIONS AND FUTURE WORK
--------------------------------------------------

  Limitations of the current study:
    - Single product type (one die, one device)
    - Fixed supply chain topology (2 FE, 1 DB, 2 BE)
    - Yield modeled as uniform between bounds (not empirical)
    - Demand uncertainty limited to two levels (600, 900)
    - Single planning period (no multi-period dynamics)

  Future work directions:
    - Multi-product extension (multiple die types and devices)
    - Empirical yield distributions from fab data
    - Capacity investment decisions (long-term planning)
    - Demand uncertainty as a third dimension of the decision map
    - Larger-scale instances to test computational scalability
